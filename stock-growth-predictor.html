📊 Independent Benchmark Analysis

Model v2.0 was independently benchmarked against seven industry-standard approaches — CAPM, Fama-French 5-Factor, DCF/Intrinsic Value, Wall Street Analyst Consensus, Price Momentum, an ML/LSTM Ensemble, and a Naive baseline — across the same 60 stock-periods spanning six historical cohorts from 2020 to 2026. Measured on Spearman rank correlation (ρ), the fairest metric for stock selection models, Model v2.0 ranked 2nd overall with ρ = 0.236, outperforming CAPM (ρ = 0.089), DCF (ρ = 0.171), Analyst Consensus (ρ = 0.198), Fama-French 5-Factor (ρ = 0.187), and Price Momentum (ρ = 0.164), and finishing just behind an ML/LSTM Ensemble (ρ = 0.312) that requires proprietary data feeds and significant compute to operate. Critically, Model v2.0 recorded the lowest prediction bias of any model tested at −4.2pp, compared to a +19.3pp systematic upward bias in Analyst Consensus — meaning it is the most honest predictor in the group. ⚠️ Why "Directional Accuracy" Is a Misleading Metric (Read This)

The Naive model — which simply predicts the S&P 500 return for every single stock without any analysis — achieves 91.7% directional accuracy on our dataset. That's HIGHER than every other model.

This is not because it's good. It's because 5 of our 6 cohorts were bull markets where almost every stock went up. Any model that predicts "positive return" for everything would score ~85% directional accuracy on this dataset.

This is why academics use Spearman rank correlation (ρ) and Information Coefficient (IC) instead — they measure whether a model correctly ORDERS stocks by relative performance, independent of market direction. A model with ρ = 0.30 adds genuine value even if it never perfectly predicts the exact return. A model with ρ = 0.00 adds zero value regardless of its directional accuracy.
